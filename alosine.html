Juvenile Alosine Ecology in the Kennebec River

Diet Analysis • Otolith Aging

I worked with Dr. Karen Wilson in the Aquatic Systems Lab at the University of Southern Maine, where I contributed to ongoing research examining habitat use by juvenile alosines in the Kennebec River system. This broader project investigates how river connectivity, dam presence, water temperature, and habitat availability influence the ecology of anadromous fish throughout their life cycle. Through otolith aging, stable isotope analysis, and diet analysis, researchers seek to better understand how these species move through and utilize freshwater, estuarine, and coastal habitats.

After several semesters in the lab, I was awarded an Undergraduate Research Fellowship to pursue an independent project focused on the feeding ecology of juvenile American shad (Alosa sapidissima) and alewife (Alosa pseudoharengus). Through stomach content analysis, I compared the diets of these two closely related species to explore whether differences in feeding strategy could reveal patterns of habitat use and resource partitioning. Although both species occupy similar ecological roles and often occur within the same environments, I was interested in whether subtle differences in prey selection could provide insight into how they interact with their habitats and coexist within the Kennebec River system.

The findings from this project were presented at the University of Southern Maine's Thinking Matters Symposium in April 2025 (presentation slides), where I shared preliminary evidence of dietary differences between juvenile alewife and American shad and discussed the broader ecological implications of trophic differentiation within the Kennebec River system.

Although my project represented only a small piece of a much larger and ongoing research effort, I did observe subtle differences in feeding patterns between the two species. American shad appeared to consume a greater proportion of benthic prey, while alewife fed more heavily on organisms associated with surface waters. While these findings are preliminary, they suggest that even closely related species occupying similar habitats may utilize available resources in different ways.

One of the most rewarding aspects of this project was learning freshwater macroinvertebrate identification. Using field guides, reference collections, and the New Hampshire Aquatic Macroinvertebrate Identification Key, I identified prey items to the lowest taxonomic level possible. This process allowed me to connect individual prey species with their ecological roles and infer feeding behavior from the contents of each stomach sample.

Beyond the specific findings, this project taught me how ecological questions are investigated through careful observation, data collection, and interpretation. It also reinforced my interest in understanding how species interact with their habitats and how those relationships change across space and time. While my studies ultimately led me in other directions during my undergraduate career, I am excited to follow the progress of this work and to see what new questions and discoveries emerge from the Kennebec River system in the years ahead.
