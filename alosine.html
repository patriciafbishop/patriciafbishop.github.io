Alosine Ecology

Juvenile Fish Ecology • Diet Analysis • Stable Isotopes

Overview

Juvenile alosines occupy a critical stage in the life cycle of anadromous fish. My research focused on understanding how alewife (Alosa pseudoharengus) and American shad (Alosa sapidissima) use habitats throughout the Kennebec River system. Through stomach content analysis, stable isotope analysis, and otolith-derived age information, this work explored how feeding ecology and habitat use vary among species and environments.

My Role

As an undergraduate researcher in the Aquatic Systems Laboratory at the University of Southern Maine, I participated in biological sample processing, stomach content analysis, data organization, and interpretation of ecological patterns observed across habitats. I also presented findings at the Thinking Matters Symposium and the Maine Soil and Water Conservation Conference.

Research Questions

How do juvenile alewife and American shad differ in their feeding ecology? How does habitat influence prey availability and resource use? What can diet and stable isotope data reveal about habitat connectivity and ecosystem function within the Kennebec River watershed?

Methods

Research methods included stomach content analysis, stable isotope analysis, otolith preparation, and habitat-based comparisons among riverine, tidal river, estuarine, and Merrymeeting Bay sampling locations.

Why It Matters

Anadromous fish serve as important ecological links between marine and freshwater ecosystems. Understanding habitat use during juvenile life stages can inform fisheries management, habitat restoration efforts, and conservation planning throughout the Gulf of Maine region.
